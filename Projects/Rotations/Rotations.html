Backend Math Code

Problem: Visualization of Rotation Parameterization, including interactivity, meant that the data & math function need to be as accessible as possible.
Not a Solution: While creating wrappers for the unity-inbuild-classes would have been possible, unity doesn't support all parameterizations directly (e.g. no rotation-vector-class).
Solution: Thus, for the sake of consistency and more control, I decided to write my own math library, meaning I had to research all the mathematical functions & implement them.
Example below: The following scroll-container shows my workflow exemplified based on my research and implementation of the exponential map of quaternions.


Step 1 — Research: For a full overview I read not only game dev sources, but also math sources.
image-legend: θ is the angle of a rotation, v is the axis of rotation. The exponential map forms a quaternion from a rotation vector: the first three quaternion entries are the rotation-axis scaled by cos(θ/2), and the fourth is sin(θ/2).
full-source: Grassia 1998, Practical Parameterization of Rotation https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~spiff/moedit99/expmap.pdf

Mathematics Visualisation

Problem: Visualise multiple different parameterizations of rotations.
Solution: Start from sketches how to visualise, then build rotation-independent math-visualisation object; Lastly piece them together.
Example below: Focuses mostly on Euler angles visualized as gimbal.

Step 1 — Inspiration: For Euler angles I found a youtube-video explaining gimbal-lock, which served as my reference & inspiration.
Original Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zc8b2Jo7mno

Step 2 — Planning: I start projects by sketching visuals and noting to-dos.
Above: Plans for 3 visualizations (quaternions and axis-angle share one). Runtime gizmos for in-scene parameter manipulation - the dark-blue-curved arrows in the axis-angle and matrix visualization - were initially planned but deprioritized.

Step 3 — Modeling: Though I have worked with proprietary modeling software (maya) before, I decided that for this project I learn blender to have a comparison to a freeware modeling program.

Step 4 — Blender-To-Unity I had to export the meshes from blender and import to Unity.
Problem: Blenders & Unity's coordinate system are different. The default blender exporter rotates whole objects, but not individual meshes. Exporting each mesh as its own .fbx would have been too much work. Thus, the blender exporter was no option.
Solution: I wrote my own blender-to-unity exporter, using matrices to convert the coordinate systems.

Step 5 — Unity-Scene-Hierarchy In Unity the unity scene hierarchy the gimbal rings are nested. The Parent-Object RotVis_EulerGimbal has the script "RotVis_EulerAngle" that references the individual elements (top of inspector), the settings (middle of inspector) and accesses the underlying Parameterization (bottom of the inspector)

Step 6 — Shaders: Since I like seeing visual networks, I used shader-graph for the angle-shaders. However, to keep it more orderly, I wrote the more complicated math functions as custom-hlsl-functions. e.g., IsValueInModRange calculates whether an angle is between two other angles respecting the modular nature of angles.

Example A rotation-shader during bug-testing.

Step 7 — Access Shaders: To test the shader (& for reusability) its values must be manipulatable from an individual gimbal-ring. However, once the full gimbal is assembled, it should be the central hub for setting up the colors of so that I don't have to continuously switch between the individual gimbal-ring-gameObjects when I adjust the colours.
